Abstract

Monitoring blood glucose levels is critical for a diabetic patient. To ensure that glycaemic control is obtained, there are a range of anti-diabetic treatments which cannot restore the lost functional beta-cell mass but can help in the management of glycaemia. These treatments include monitoring of the blood glucose levels via skin mounted patches i.e. transdermal applications and subcutaneous methods such as injecting insulin into a diabetic person. Meanwhile, chemists and engineers alike are investigating other approaches such as oral, nasal and buccal applications. Given the evolving nature of diabetes, there is an increase in research of treatments and a large variety of therapeutic approaches have been proposed. Despite the struggle in monitoring diabetes, engineers have proposed many ways in which alleviate the “disturbances” that may come with managing diabetes.

Introduction: What is diabetes?

Diabetes is a metabolic disease defined by elevated blood glucose levels. It is comprised of four types: type 1, type 2, pre-diabetes and gestational diabetes, with type 1 and 2 accounting for ~95% of diagnosed forms worldwide. Type 1 diabetes involves autoimmune destruction of beta-cells which produce insulin, whereas type 2 diabetes involves insulin resistance where beta-cells are either lost or impaired. If untreated this can result in hyperglycaemia (causing cardiovascular and neurological complications) or hypoglycaemia (leading to fatigue and possibly premature death).

The worldwide distribution of diabetes predicted by the WHO suggests ~700 million people affected by 2045 (Diabetes rising worldwide: 1 in 11 adults affected (2019)).

1. Monitoring diabetes: Cause and prevention

Blood glucose levels can be monitored via the meal a patient consumes, proving to be a major disturbance in varying blood glucose levels.

To be diagnosed as diabetic, a person’s blood glucose level must meet criteria based on clinical testing:

  • Fasting plasma glucose test (FPG): fasting for 8 h before the test.
  • Oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT): patient consumes 75 g glucose syrup; blood glucose measured after 2 hours.
  • Glycated haemoglobin (HbA1c): lab measurement of average blood glucose over 3 months.

Type 2 diabetes accounts for ~90% of the population in the UK (Khan, R. et al. (2019)), strongly associated with obesity.

Studies reveal that BMI, waist circumference, sedentary lifestyle, stress, smoking, alcohol consumption and sleep patterns all play roles in diabetes risk. Research also shows hypertension and diabetes may share metabolic origins linked to obesity (Cheung & Li, 2012).

2. Treating diabetes: drug delivery and development

Home blood glucose tests such as glucometers require a finger prick blood sample. Environmental factors may affect accuracy. Modern alternatives include glucose-sensing patches, insulin pumps, buccal delivery systems, oral spray insulin and subcutaneous injections.

Subcutaneous insulin injections remain the most common delivery method, but recent innovations such as “ultra-rapid acting” insulin aim to reduce hypoglycaemia risks and improve glycaemic control.

Conclusions

Despite diabetes being one of the most prevalent diseases worldwide, prevention can be achieved through healthy lifestyle behaviours. Ongoing research continues to explore improved insulin delivery methods that maintain rapid action while reducing discomfort. This article provides insight into diabetes, its causes, treatments and how engineering developments may improve therapeutic outcomes.
